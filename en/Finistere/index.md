# Finistère

The (unfinished) text below is a draft I started writing as an attempt to distill experiences, discoveries and learnings happening between the (European) summer of 2025 and spring 2026. During that time I traveled to different places, among which Finistère, in France. I was there for the first residency (or rather, immersion - even literally) of the [Floating Stories Lab
](https://www.floatingstorieslab.org/).

Even though the text mentions other trips and events happening before and after that short stay in Finistère, I'm pasting it here for a couple of reasons. First of all, it can be seen as a sort of output of my time there. More subtly, though, if I ever finish this text the idea is to position it to denote the end of a period in my life. More precisely, I expect this to be the last part of my journal Future Beyond Repair and transition into a new life cycle.

And what does that have to do with Finistère? Well, it may be that the sight of the reflection of the sun over the ocean when I was being driven to Plouhinec was the first time I considered the new phase of my life that will start in June 2026. And this text was written under that liminal feeling of being at the edge. Not anymore stable as firm earth, not yet swiftly sailing the high seas. The edge, the coast, the border between earth and water. 

There it goes - and I'll need to come back here to keep writing it. Or delete it, who knows? That is the nature of water.

# The end of earth is water

	O fim da terra é água.
	Da lama ao cosmos, mangue-ficção, redes de afetos.
	Mapas, tradições, saberes.
	Permear a preia-mar.

Things come in cycles, and yet rarely happen exactly the same. 

---

A good part of my work in recent years revolved around reflecting and experimenting - in conceptual and practical terms - about cycles. At a superficial level, the literal presence of cycles and circles in the most material of my research topics. Re-cycling - or, better said, postponing waste disposal through the reuse of materials - and circular economy (albeit from an angle of fairness and inclusivity) are obvious examples. But the notions used for instance in co-creating the [semente toolkit](https://semente.de) may be a more nuanced take on circularity and cycles. That is, not only departing from linear thinking (inspired by, among others, Le Guin), but a spiralled composition merging linearity with cyclical development (influenced for instance by the late Germano Afonso by way of Fabi Borges).

In other words, instead of expecting to construct closed circles eternally repeating themselves, the sort of approach described here is one of combining awareness of recurrence with attention to how conditions do change at each turn - or don't. The solstice comes once again. It's not the same solstice as last year's, and nonetheless it's significantly similar to that one. And possibly to the next one, to the extent that it will come.

It is completely feasible to merge linearity and circularity when experiencing the cycles of life. To watch things repeat and diverge continuously and in varied rhythms, and still find meaning - a complex, challenging one. And yet, one can intentionally decide to break away from such cycles. Just as much as noting that healthy communities are often constituted by the tension between identity and difference, likewise reflecting on how time passes through and how we pass through time may offer a richer perspective about change, how it occurs, and where our agency lies therein. Sometimes it's best to just wait and lower all immediate expectations. Observe, hunker down, be strategic... conditions are not yet ripe. And suddenly, the time to act comes - time to move, accelerate, diverge, create universes anew. Instead of imposing change by force, it's about flowing and adaptin according to each moment and what it may offer.

Many cultural and spiritual traditions around the world bring important wisdom on these subjects. I won't enumerate references here, but a common thread I'm currently interested in is how _water_ acts as a metaphor for ways of being and living in the world. This text is an attempt to summarise thoughts and recent events in that direction.

## A Benjaminian nod

In July 2025, I had the pleasure of partaking the initial days of [NØ SCHOOL NEVERS](https://www.noschoolnevers.com/2025.html).  My participation was quite short, as I was on my way to other commitments. It was inspirational all the same. In addition to the time engaged with workshops and conversations with students, teachers and team, I also did a small ritualistic intervention at the margins of the Loire.

Almost ten years ago I was in Nantes - almost at the other end of that river, near its estuary. The longest river in France, _La Loire_ cuts through the geography and history of France. More to the interior of France, the Loire valley and its beautiful palaces tell stories of luxury while keeping inequality and colonial exploitation outside the postal cards. Nantes taught me about its shipbuilding past, as well as the role of the city in the horrific slave trade of centuries past. In Nevers, the riverbank has a beach - quite convenient to refresh from the extremely hot days we were having in July. The pedestrian path to the beach crosses a long bridge over the Loire. As a way to travel through geography and back in time, I made a small paper plane to my friend Julien Bellanger. He lives near Nantes, hundreds of kilometers from there, and I hadn't seen him in almost ten years. I asked La Loire to take the message in his direction.

![LOIRE]

A couple of days before that, having just arrived in Nevers, I was being driven by [Benjamin Gaulon](https://recyclism.com/index.html) to the event's shared accommodation. Ben is as great a host as he is a co-coordinator - together with [Dasha Ilina](https://dashailina.com/) - of NØSCHOOL. From the back seat of the car waiting for a traffic light to open, I read a street sign - "Esplanade Walter Benjamin" - and noticed the parallelism of being driven by a Benjamin along the Benjamin square. Later I'd visit the Esplanade and find that Walter Benjamin had been incarcerated in the region after the Nazi regime stripped German Jews of their citizenship rights. W Benjamin had been born in Berlin, and at the time of his imprisonment, he was about as old as I am today. His would be a sad ending not even one year later, in an attempt to flee war-torn Europe through Spain. There's a [memorial in Portbou](https://www.fundacioangelusnovus.org/en/memorial/), Catalunya, to remember his tragedy.

I brought that up during my class in Nevers. Afterwards, one of the students asked me to describe my work. My response came in the usual non-committing tone, something to the effect of "I write emails and visit people, and I collect stories and stuff". In saying that, awareness of Benjamin loomed through my mind. The next day, another student asked what I liked most about Benjamin's writings, and the image of the ragpicker came to mind. The original term in German is arguably richer - the _Lumpensammler_ - that is, a collector - not only picker - of rags. For Benjamin, the ragpicker is a sort of curator of debris, of material excess, of the modernity's leftovers and refuse, interpreting humanity through its materiality. Ragpickers collect stories and things while simultaneously discarding - frequently ignoring - other things and other stories. The short days in Nevers made me conscious of how that is also present in my work.

The journey after Nevers continued during many hours on train rides, coffee shops, other events and inspiring conversations through five other cities, before I came back to the place I then called home. All that time, I was trying to distil thoughts into something more structured. An artwork, a research project, a piece of software, a methodology. I brainstormed titles and names relating to some of those recent topics - the Benjaminian ragpicker - *Lumpensammler*, *chiffonier*, *trapeiro*, *drapaire*. From another perspective, there was also the thing with water perhaps awakened by the Loire. And for that I'll need to break the linearity of this text by jumping further back in time.

## On water as a metaphor

Through the worst parts of the most recent far-right phase in Brazil - in the year that Bolsonaro almost got re-elected for president - I used water as an analogy to what we were trying to achieve with the *semente* toolkit. To put it briefly, I described the early thriving days of the digital culture movement in my country as a tropical rainforest, and the years dominated by the political right as a desert. The reader can imagine what water would mean in that context.

The following project developed with Instituto Neos and supported by the University of Bristol was aptly called "fonte.wiki". Fonte is an interesting word in Portuguese. It can be translated as "water fountain" or spring. But it can also be translated as "source" - as in water source - as much as source as an origin. In terms of software, "source code" is usually translated as "código-fonte".
